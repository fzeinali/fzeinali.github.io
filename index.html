About

I am a fourth-year PhD candidate in Industrial Engineering at Clemson University. Before starting my PhD, I studied industrial engineering and worked in industry, which shaped my interest in solving real operational problems using quantitative and data-driven approaches.

I enjoy working on problems where data, modeling, and computation can help improve how systems operate and support better decision-making. I am particularly interested in translating complex systems into clear analytical models and decision support tools that organizations can use to understand their operations and make more informed decisions.

Research

My research focuses on developing data-driven methods to improve decision-making and operational performance in complex systems. I integrate operations research, simulation modeling, machine learning, and data analytics to understand system dynamics, predict demand, and evaluate alternative strategies before they are implemented in practice.

My work emphasizes translating real-world operational problems into quantitative models that support better planning and resource allocation. Using large-scale data and computational modeling, I analyze how system design, operational policies, and uncertainty influence performance metrics such as efficiency, waiting times, service levels, and resource utilization.

A key component of my research is the development of decision-support tools that combine predictive analytics, simulation, and optimization. These tools allow organizations to evaluate scenarios, anticipate future demand, and test operational strategies in a virtual environment, enabling more informed and proactive decision-making in complex systems.
